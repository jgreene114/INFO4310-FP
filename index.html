The 2024 Atlantic Hurricane Season

How bad could it be?

Scientists predict an intense 2024 Atlantic Hurricane Season

Researchers at Colorado State University predict with "above-normal confidence... that the 2024 Atlantic basin hurricane season will be extremely active." (CITE) They state that with the current El Niño trend and record warm Atlantic sea surface temperatures (SST) there is "a well above-average probability for major hurricanes making landfall along the continental United States coastline and in the Caribbean." (CITE)

What is a hurricane?

Tropical cyclone terminology and trends (CITE)

Hurricanes are the most violent storms on Earth and are a type of tropical cyclone that forms over the Atlantic Ocean or eastern Pacific Ocean. Depending on their location, tropical cyclones may be called hurricanes, typhoons or cyclones. All these storms are scientifically categorized under the term "tropical cyclone." The defining features of a hurricane include intense swirling winds, a calm center known as the "eye," and heavy rainfall. (CITE)

How do hurricanes form?

Hurricanes form from the energy and moisture provided by warm ocean waters near the equator. The process begins when warm, moist air over the ocean rises, creating an area of lower air pressure beneath it. As this air rises, cooler air from surrounding areas flows into the low-pressure area, warms up, becomes moist, and rises as well. This cycle causes the air to swirl due to the Earth's rotation—counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere. (CITE)













As the warm air cools, it releases its moisture, forming storm clouds. These clouds and winds continue to rotate and organize into bands around the eye of the storm. The storm strengthens as it draws more heat and moisture from the ocean. When wind speeds within this rotating system reach 74 mph, it is classified as a hurricane. The storm system features a calm, clear eye at its center, surrounded by a wall of intense winds and heavy clouds, known as the eyewall. (CITE)

How do hurricanes form?

"There are several favorable environmental conditions that must be in place before a tropical cyclone can form. They are:

  • Warm ocean waters (at least 80°F / 27°C) throughout a depth of about 150 ft. (46 m).
  • An atmosphere which cools fast enough with height such that it is potentially unstable to moist convection.
  • Relatively moist air near the mid-level of the troposphere (16,000 ft. / 4,900 m).
  • Generally, a minimum distance of at least 300 miles (480 km) from the equator.
  • A pre-existing near-surface disturbance.
  • Low values (less than about 23 mph / 37 km/h) of vertical wind shear between the surface and the upper troposphere."
(CITE)









"Tropical cyclones are classified by their maximum wind speed.

  • Winds less than 39 mph: Tropical depressions
  • Winds 39-73 mph: Tropical storms
  • Winds 74 mph or greater: Hurricanes
  • Major hurricanes have winds of at least 111 mph and can reach speeds of over 180 mph, with gusts of 200 mph."
(CITE)

Hurricane Katrina First Hitting Land

Hurricane Katrina first made landfall on August 25, 2005, near Miami, Florida, as a Category 1 hurricane. This initial landfall brought heavy rain and strong winds to the region, causing flooding and power outages across South Florida. After moving across the state, Katrina entered the warm waters of the Gulf of Mexico, where it rapidly intensified into a much stronger storm.

Katrina's first encounter with land was a critical moment in its development. The storm's passage over Florida did not significantly weaken it due to the relatively short duration over land and the warm Gulf waters waiting on the other side. This set the stage for its rapid intensification and eventual catastrophic impact when it made its second and more devastating landfall near Buras, Louisiana, a few days later. (CITE)

Where do hurricanes form?

Tropical cyclone formation basins (CITE)

"Given that sea surface temperatures need to be at least 80°F (27°C) for tropical cyclones to form, it is unsurprising that they form near the equator. However, with only the rarest exceptions, these storms do not form within 5° latitude of the equator. This is due to the lack of sufficient Coriolis force, the force that causes the cyclone to spin. With the above criteria, there are seven regions around the world where tropical cyclones are likely to form.



North Atlantic Ocean, the Gulf of Mexico, and the Caribbean Sea
The hurricane season is officially from June 1 to November 30. Peak activity is in early to mid September. Occasionally, there may be a tropical cyclone that occurs in May or December.


Northeast Pacific basin Mexico to about the dateline
A broad peak with activity beginning in late May or early June and going until late October or early November, with a peak in storminess in late August/early September.


Australian/Southwest Pacific basin 142°E to about 120°W
Begins in late October/early November, reaches a single peak in late February/early March, and then fades out in early May.


Northwest Pacific basin From the dateline to Asia, including the South China Sea
Occur regularly all year, although there is a distinct minimum in February and the first half of March. The main season goes from July to November with a peak in late August/early September.


North Indian basin Including the Bay of Bengal and the Arabian Sea
Tropical cyclones occur from April to December, with a double peak of activity in May and November. The severe cyclonic storms (>74 mph / 119 km/h winds) occur almost exclusively from April to June and again in late September to early December.


Southwest Indian basin From Africa to about 100°E
Late October/early November to May, with a double peak in activity in mid-January and mid-February/early March.


Southeast Indian/Australian basin 100°E to 142°E
Late October/early November to May, with a double peak in activity in mid-January and mid-February/early March. A lull in activity in February is a bit more pronounced than the Southwest Indian basin's lull."
(CITE)






Main Development Region (MDR)

While Katrina did not start in the MDR, understanding this region is crucial for grasping how many hurricanes intensify. The Main Development Region (MDR) of the North Atlantic Ocean runs from Africa to the Caribbean Islands and is a prime area for tropical cyclone formation. This region is annually home to some of the warmest waters, allowing tropical cyclones to develop quickly during the heart of the hurricane season.

Large-scale climate patterns such as the El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO), the Atlantic Meridional Mode (AMM), and the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) can impact how active the MDR is during a season. A La Niña cycle of the ENSO, a positive status of the AMM, and a warm phase of the AMO enhance tropical cyclone development in this region.

Typically, the first hurricane of the season forms in the MDR in August, with the first major cyclone appearing in September. Interestingly, four of the top five costliest U.S. hurricanes did not become significant cyclones in the MDR, indicating that while the region is a hotspot for hurricane development, it is not a necessity for a cyclone to become a powerful disaster. (CITE)

Impact of Rising Sea Surface Temperatures 1

Rising ocean surface temperatures are a critical factor in the increasing intensity of hurricanes. As the planet warms, primarily due to human-induced climate change, ocean temperatures rise, which directly influences hurricane behavior. (CITE)

Impact of Rising Sea Surface Temperatures 2

Warmer ocean waters fuel hurricanes by increasing the rate of evaporation. This enhanced evaporation leads to higher humidity and more water vapor available in the atmosphere. Water vapor acts as a potent source of energy for hurricanes, intensifying their strength and enabling them to sustain stronger winds and produce heavier rainfall. (CITE)

How warm water fuels hurricanes (CITE)

Impact of Rising Sea Surface Temperatures 3

Consequently, as sea surface temperatures continue to climb, the power and potential destructiveness of hurricanes are expected to increase. This trend is vividly demonstrated by the rising sea surface temperature data from 1982 to 2024, with 2024 showing a notably higher temperature trend compared to the average of previous years. This ongoing increase in sea surface temperatures is a clear signal of how climate dynamics are shifting, potentially leading to more frequent and severe weather events. (CITE)

Katrina Reaches Category 5 Strength

On August 28, 2005, Hurricane Katrina reached its peak intensity as a Category 5 hurricane with sustained winds of 175 mph (280 km/h). This intensification was fueled by the extremely warm waters of the Gulf of Mexico, with SSTs reaching 30°C (86°F), and low vertical wind shear that allowed the storm to organize and strengthen efficiently.

Key Information:

  • Wind Speed: 175 mph (280 km/h)

  • Central Pressure: 902 millibars (hPa)

  • Size: Katrina's wind field was massive, with hurricane-force winds extending 90 miles (150 km) from the center and tropical-storm-force winds reaching up to 200 miles (320 km) outward.

  • Impact: As a Category 5 hurricane, Katrina posed a catastrophic threat to the Gulf Coast. Its surge and winds were capable of overwhelming levee systems, causing widespread flooding and damage.

(CITE)
